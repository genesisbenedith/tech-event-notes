## Overview

This panel brought together industry and youth voices to explore findings from the Girls Who Code + UCLA Computer Science Equity Project study, investigating how teens and young adults — particularly girls and women — make sense of AI and its impacts on their identities, futures, and actions. The discussion aimed to translate youth-informed research into actionable insights for responsible AI development, equitable AI education, and workforce readiness.

### AI Ethics & Governance

- **IBM** named their AI ethics bylaws: **Risk Atlas**
- **Anthropic** named their AI ethics bylaws: **The Constitution**
  - Both are constantly amended
  - Principles drawn from the **United Nations** and the **United States Constitution**

- **Ethics cannot be an add-on or a feature**
  - Who does the impact exclude?
  - What should AI *not* be doing?

- **Train your data on ethics**
- **Add more week-to-week evaluations** — add/enhance more security protocols
- **Be accountable for your own AI**
- ML was used by the last generation to predict whether consumers should be approved for a credit card — and **there was bias in that**

- > *"Top 1% ChatGPT user"* → lost authenticity in her writing → stepped away from AI more → heavy use loses voice, authenticity, and creativity
  - *(Insight shared by a panelist from personal experience)*

- **Girls Who Code program** increased positive outlooks, increased interest/engagement, and enhanced perspectives on AI
- **Data/AI ethics IS data/AI governance** — and vice versa. They are the same thing.

### AI in Education — Backlash & Concerns

- **Recent backlash at commencements** when AI was the focus of graduation speeches
  - Panelists discussed giving students **more opportunities to learn AI + ML**

- **Skepticism around "human in the loop"**
  - Writing assessments graded by AI — what are the statistics on LLM grading accuracy?
    - Related tools/concerns: Gradescope, plagiarism checkers, AI checkers, AI content creators, academic integrity, and academic standards

> 📝 **Personal Note:** Eric Schmidt (former CEO of Google) was a commencement speaker at the University of Arizona (my alma mater). There was significant backlash — CS graduates who struggled to find jobs and were prohibited from using AI throughout their degree found it tone-deaf to hear that AI would now touch everything. This tension — between AI being banned in academic settings and then being positioned as the future of every industry — is worth exploring further. The lack of AI literacy and AI fluency being built during undergrad, while simultaneously being told it's the future, is a real gap worth addressing.
